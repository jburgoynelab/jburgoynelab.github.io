Research Insterest
Redox Biology

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) are ubiquitously generated from numerous intracellular processes. Although originally considered as purely determinantal due to their ability to directly modify susceptible biomolecules, the formation of ROS it is now well recognised as an important mediator of normal physiological signalling. This is largely through their ability to selectively modify susceptible protein thiols, with the end-product often being the formation of a reversible disulphide, either within the same protein (intramolecular), between chains (intermolecular) or with the cellular replete tripeptide glutathione (S-glutathionylation).

To unravel the intricate processes that regulate redox signalling, it is our primary goal to identify and characterise protein cysteine thiols susceptible to redox-dependent reversible disulphide formation, as these represent important mediators of signalling but also key targets for therapy. To identify targets susceptible to redox-dependent reversible disulphide formation we use proteomics and structural bioinformatics. Candidates identified using these approaches are then validated and characterised using biochemical, molecular and cell biology techniques, with the physiological and pathological significance of protein modification then determined using relevant cell and animal models. Targets of reversible redox-dependent intermolecular disulphide formation, identified using these approaches include the cGMP-dependent protein kinase 1 alpha (PKG1a) and the autophagy related enzymes Atg3/Atg7. Characterisation of these targets led to the discovery that PKG1a disulphide dimerisation is a key regulator of blood pressure (Burgoyne et al, Science 2007), whereas Atg3/Atg7 oxidation during aging leads to impairment of the cytoprotective process of autophagy (Frudd et al, Nature Communications 2018).

By targeting the site of oxidation in PKG1a we have identified the first of a novel class of antihypertensive (Burgoyne et al, Hypertension 2017), as well as showing that the antioxidant resveratrol mediates its blood pressure lowering effect by inducing kinase disulphide dimerisation (Prysyazhna et al, Circulation 2019). Although the ability of the antioxidant resveratrol to induce thiol oxidation may seem counterintuitive, its ability to redox cycle leads to formation of a reactive quinone intermediate that can modify cysteine. This ability of resveratrol to induce thiol oxidation is likely to be common amongst many antioxidants that share similar chemistry. Therefore, this discovery highlights a new paradigm in antioxidant signalling, by explaining how these molecules may mediate many of their beneficial effects through direct thiol oxidation.

As well as identifying, characterising and targeting site of thiol oxidation, a new area of interest is in studying novel epigenetic process that contribute to cardiovascular health and disease. In particular, in identifying and characterising novel histone epigenetic modifications that may contribute to cardiac remodelling and the development of heart failure.
